The problem

A high-stakes workflow running on tribal knowledge

The Accounting Controls Group (ACG) manages the financial rules that determine how every transaction maps to a general ledger output. When a rule needs to change — a new product, a regulatory update, a business restructure — a request enters their queue. ACG reviews it, builds the change, gets it approved, and deploys it.

In practice, none of that happened in one place. Requests arrived through Jira, almost always incomplete. Clarification threads stretched across comments and messages. Rule logic was built manually with no shared reference. Approvals were tracked informally. The knowledge of how to do any of it correctly lived in specific people — not the system.

"The back-and-forth is the work. We're not slow — we're blocked before we even start."

The first six months were spent mapping this reality through stakeholder workshops and secondary research, without direct user access. When user research began in January 2025, it pressure-tested those assumptions against actual ACG workflows — and reshaped the design direction in the process.

Why this was uniquely complex

This wasn't a form redesign

Enterprise workflow problems often look simple from the outside. This one had compounding constraints that made surface-level solutions fail immediately.

01 8-stage workflow across 3 actor groups with no shared system of record
02 High governance sensitivity — rules directly impact financial outputs and audit trails
03 Tribal knowledge risk — critical logic lived in people, not documentation
04 Business users not yet in the platform — design had to bridge two worlds simultaneously
05 Development building screen-by-screen without a shared workflow model
06 No user access in Phase 1 — design had to be grounded in stakeholder synthesis, then validated
The system

Map the workflow before designing the screens

Before a single screen was designed, the end-to-end workflow had to be documented. Development had been building components in isolation — technically correct but disconnected from the flow around them. Business stakeholders kept rejecting builds not because the screens were wrong, but because no one had mapped how they connected.

The workflow map became the most consequential design artifact on the project — not a UI component, but a shared model that reoriented the team around user goals rather than ticket completion.

The design

Request details — a workspace, not a page

The request details surface is the core of the ACG workflow — where they verify, classify, build, and ultimately approve rule changes. Rather than a static detail view, it was designed as a dynamic workspace that responds to where the request is in the workflow.

Role context
This view belongs to an ACG team member who can update rules and act on requests assigned to them — but cannot reassign requests or access other teams' work. The locked and unlocked states aren't just workflow sequencing. They reflect what this user is permitted to do at this stage. Permissions are enforced by the system, not by convention.

Key decisions

What shaped the design

01
In-platform actions drive workflow state — not manual status updates
Each stage advances because someone completed a real piece of work. Classifying a request to a rule completes Verification. Saving the cloned rule change completes Execution. No one drags a status card. Live rules are never edited directly — ACG always works on a clone with a defined effective date, so the original stays active until the replacement is ready to deploy. The activity feed is the audit trail that makes this visible.
02
Workflow-first, not screen-first
The most impactful design work wasn't a component or a layout — it was documenting the eight-stage workflow and getting engineering and stakeholders aligned on it before building continued. Dev had been producing technically correct screens in the wrong sequence. Business kept rejecting them. The workflow map gave the team a shared model to build against and reframed UX as the function that maintained the end-to-end view.
03
Designed for a two-sided platform, built for one side first
Business users aren't in the platform yet — they receive a structured email summary to validate before deployment. The design explicitly acknowledges this in the activity feed rather than hiding it. The architecture is already built for them to enter the platform when the time comes. The validation surface, the handoff sequence, the visibility into rule changes — it's all there. The bridge is temporary. The destination is designed.
What shifted

From screen delivery to workflow alignment

The measurable outcomes of this project aren't in analytics — they're in how the team thinks and works. Those are often the harder changes to make in an enterprise environment.

Team alignment
Dev shifted from screen-by-screen to workflow-first after the journey documentation was introduced. Business rejection rate on builds dropped significantly.
Design governance
UX established as a formal sign-off gate in the development process — documented in Jira story requirements, not just assumed.
Research foundation
5 user interviews reoriented the design from stakeholder assumptions to actual ACG workflows, surfacing gaps that hadn't been documented anywhere in the organisation.
